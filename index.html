Early Career Researchers (ECRs)
More willing than senior researchers to accept invitations, especially from high-impact indexed journals (SCI/SSCI). They see it as building expertise and credibility. But they're less confident and often lack formal training. A sense of extra-role responsibility drives them more than it does senior peers.
Industry Researchers — The Untapped Pool
Industry-based researchers consistently report receiving fewer invitations and having more time available to review than academic counterparts. Publishers who want to expand their reviewer pool have a clear, underused source here.

🔍
They Vet the Journal
Before accepting, experienced reviewers check the journal's impact factor, publishing standards, and ethical reputation. An unfamiliar journal is a fast path to decline. Reviewer guidelines and AI policies are also now part of this checklist.
⚖️
They Check for Conflict
Reviewers assess if they know the authors, have a competing financial interest, or have a prior opinion that would bias their review. This conflict-of-interest check is part of formal COPE guidance.
⏰
Time is the Most Honest Filter
Even reviewers who want to say yes will decline if the deadline is tight. The standard turnaround requested by journals is 14–21 days; shorter windows dramatically increase declination rates.
Sources: IOP Publishing State of Peer Review 2024; Publons Global State of Peer Review 2018; COPE Reviewer Guidelines; multiple journal-level survey studies.
Section 03

The Review Process, End to End

What actually happens from the moment an invitation lands to when the review reaches the editor. Each stage carries its own friction and failure points.

Stage 1 · Editorial
Editor assigns the manuscript
An editor-in-chief, associate editor, or managing editor is assigned. They identify suitable reviewers — typically targeting 2–3 confirmed reviewers minimum. They send out multiple invitations simultaneously because the expected acceptance rate is low (average 5.6 invitations needed per paper).
Stage 2 · Invitation
Reviewer receives the invite
Reviewer receives an email with abstract and basic paper details. They run the mental checklist: Is this my area? Do I have time? Do I trust this journal? Do I have a conflict of interest? This decision typically takes minutes to a few days.
Stage 3 · First read
Initial impression formed
The reviewer reads the full manuscript for the first time. This read forms an overall impression. If major structural or ethical problems are immediately visible, a reviewer may recommend rejection at this stage without detailed review. Otherwise, they proceed to detailed analysis.
Stage 4 · Deep review
Detailed read and note-taking
The reviewer reads carefully, taking notes. They assess: originality, scientific significance, methodological rigor, clarity, conciseness, and completeness. They evaluate the argument's construction and whether conclusions are supported by evidence. Average time: ~3–5 hours.
Stage 5 · Report
Writing the review
The reviewer writes a structured report with: a summary of the paper (in their own words), major concerns (methodological, conceptual, ethical), minor concerns (clarity, language, references), and a recommendation: Accept / Minor Revision / Major Revision / Reject.
Stage 6 · Editorial decision
Editor synthesizes and decides
The editor reads all reviews, weighs them, and makes a decision. If reviews strongly diverge, a third reviewer may be invited. The editor may not follow reviewer recommendations — this is a common point of friction. Decision is sent to the author with reviewer comments.

"Reviewers are not given any formal training by most publishers. The skill of peer review is mostly learned informally — from supervisors, from being reviewed yourself, and from trial and error."

— IOP Publishing State of Peer Review 2024
The AI Wildcard (2024–25)
35% of researchers predict AI will have a negative impact on peer review; 29% predict positive; 36% neutral. Most major publishers (including IOP) currently prohibit using AI to write or augment review reports, citing data protection and confidentiality concerns. Enforcement is nearly impossible.
Sources: IOP Publishing 2024; COPE Reviewer Guidelines; Wiley reviewer guidance; journal-level process studies.

What Publishers Can Offer

The Rewards Spectrum

💬
Feedback on Their Review
The #1 requested reward in IOP's 2024 survey. IOP became the first major publisher to offer structured editorial feedback in 2023. ~60% of reviewers opted in. Rated overwhelmingly positively. Almost no other major publishers do this.
📋
Decision Transparency
Reviewers strongly want to know the final editorial decision on papers they reviewed, with reasoning. This closes the loop and signals that their contribution mattered. Widely requested, unevenly delivered across publishers.
🏅
Public Recognition
Named reviewer listings, certificates, and verified review records (e.g. Publons/ORCID integration) matter more than cash. Recognition creates the social standing and sense of importance that drives continued participation.
🎓
Training Access
IOP's Peer Review Excellence programme has had 6,000+ sign-ups since 2021. The 2024 survey saw "better peer review training" rise dramatically in importance rankings since 2020. ECRs especially value structured training.
💳
Fee Waivers / Journal Access
Some publishers offer APC (article processing charge) discounts or free journal access in exchange for reviews. These are moderately valued, especially in low-income country contexts where journal access is expensive.
💰
Cash Payment (Emerging)
Rated lowest but trending upward. ResearchHub Journal pays $150/review in crypto. Two other journals ran trials in 2025 with mixed results. Cash is controversial — some argue it changes reviewer motivation from intrinsic to transactional.
Sources: IOP Publishing 2024; Springer Nature reviewer testimonials; academic literature on academic labor and peer review rewards; Nature News (2025).

The Structural Crisis
Since 2018, Publons confirmed "widespread signs of peer review fatigue." Academics are now less likely to accept invitations than five years prior. Springer Nature's own 2023 Editor Satisfaction Survey (31,273 responses across 2,033 journals) found that finding reviewers who accept requests is one of the top two pain points for editors — alongside peer review systems themselves. Editors need to send 5.6 invitations on average just to secure reviewers for a single paper. This isn't reviewer laziness. It's a structural supply-demand mismatch driven by submission growth outpacing reviewer pool growth.

"Burnout is not only physical but psychological and cognitive. It often arises from engaging in activities that feel devoid of meaning — fatigue is caused not by workload alone, but by a lack of enjoyment in the work."

— Academic literature on reviewer motivation and burnout

⚠️ The Transparency Gap
Springer Nature and Taylor & Francis both now publish meaningful reviewer survey data. IOP publishes the most methodologically detailed research. But Wiley and Elsevier — two of the largest publishers — publish no equivalent public reviewer satisfaction data. This makes it nearly impossible for researchers or product teams to benchmark against them fairly. Absence of data is itself a finding.
🏆 Where Springer Nature Leads
With 236,000+ reviewer responses tracked over four years, Springer Nature now has the largest public dataset on reviewer satisfaction. Their 85–86% consistent satisfaction rate and 90% willingness-to-review-again score sets a concrete benchmark. Their 2024–25 investments in Reviewer Communities, downloadable certificates, and the annual reviewer "Year in Review" email represent the most scaled recognition programme currently operating.

"We know that reviewers face a lot of demand for their time and that reviewer fatigue is a genuine problem. It is therefore vital that Springer Nature continually invest in improving the reviewer experience."

— Marc Gillett, Director of Editorial Engagement, Springer Nature (2025)
What the Best Publishers Do
Across all publishers with public data, the reviewers who are most satisfied share a consistent set of experiences:

✓ Clear, flexible review instructions
✓ Responsive editorial communication
✓ Easy-to-use submission platform
✓ Formal recognition (certificates, listings, dashboards)
✓ Feedback on their review quality
✓ Training that matches their career stage
✓ Reviewer-paper matching that respects their expertise
The Gap No Publisher Has Solved
Across all publishers, time constraints remain the #1 reason reviewers would not review again (Springer Nature 2024; T&F 2023). No publisher has solved the structural tension: peer review is unpaid labour in a system where submissions keep rising and researchers' other demands don't shrink. The institutional incentive structure — hiring, tenure, grants — doesn't formally reward reviewing. Certificates and dashboards help at the margin. They don't fix the root cause.
